The Tradition

Why Koreans Eat This on Birthdays

In Korea, your birthday doesn't start with cake. It starts with a steaming bowl of miyeokguk — a simple, nourishing seaweed soup that carries a meaning far deeper than its humble ingredients suggest.

The tradition begins with mothers. After giving birth, Korean women eat miyeokguk for weeks — sometimes daily for 21 days or more. Miyeok (dried seaweed) is rich in iron, calcium, and iodine, nutrients that help the body recover, replenish blood, and support milk production. For centuries, it has been considered the most important postpartum food in Korean culture, believed to cleanse the body and restore a mother's strength.

So every year on your birthday, when you sit down to a bowl of miyeokguk, you are not just eating breakfast. You are honoring your mother — remembering the meal she ate to heal and nourish herself so she could nourish you. It's a quiet act of gratitude. A reminder that you exist because someone loved you enough to bring you into the world.

That's why, even today, the first thing a Korean person hears on their birthday is often not "happy birthday" but "did you eat your miyeokguk?"

The Recipe

Beef Miyeokguk

15 min
Prep
40 min
Cook
4
Servings
Easy
Difficulty

Ingredients

  • Dried miyeok (seaweed) 1 oz / 30g
  • Beef brisket or stew meat, cubed 6 oz / 170g
  • Sesame oil 1 tbsp
  • Minced garlic 1 tbsp
  • Soy sauce (soup soy sauce) 2 tbsp
  • Water 8 cups
  • Salt to taste
  • Ground black pepper pinch

Instructions

  1. Soak the seaweed. Place the dried miyeok in a large bowl of cold water for 15–20 minutes. It will expand 5–6 times its size. Drain, gently squeeze out excess water, and cut into bite-sized pieces if the strands are very long.
  2. Season the beef. Toss the cubed beef with 1 tablespoon of soy sauce and a pinch of pepper. Let it sit while the seaweed soaks.
  3. Sauté the beef. Heat sesame oil in a heavy pot over medium-high heat. Add the beef and sear until lightly browned on all sides, about 3–4 minutes.
  4. Add garlic and seaweed. Add the minced garlic and drained miyeok to the pot. Stir-fry together for 2–3 minutes until the seaweed is coated in oil and slightly translucent.
  5. Add water and simmer. Pour in 8 cups of water. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat to medium-low. Cover and simmer for 30–40 minutes. The longer it simmers, the richer and more savory the broth becomes.
  6. Season. Add the remaining 1 tablespoon of soy sauce. Taste and adjust with salt. The broth should be clean, savory, and slightly briny from the seaweed.
  7. Serve hot in deep bowls with a bowl of freshly steamed white rice on the side — this is essential. The mild, sticky rice balances the briny depth of the soup perfectly. Spoon rice directly into the soup or alternate bites. In Korea, you eat this first thing in the morning — before anything else on your birthday.
The Shortcut

The Easy Way (Still So Good)

You can buy frozen dried miyeokguk blocks at any Korean grocery store — they come pre-seasoned with seaweed and broth base, freeze-dried into a brick. That's your cheat code. Here's all you do:

  1. Sauté the beef. Cut brisket into small cubes. Heat a tablespoon of sesame oil in a pot, sear the beef until browned. Brisket (chest cut) is the classic choice — it's fatty enough to make the broth rich. Flank steak works great too. Avoid lean cuts like sirloin — you want that marbled fat melting into the soup.
  2. Add water + the frozen block. Pour in 3–4 cups of water, drop in the miyeokguk block, and bring to a boil.
  3. Simmer 10 minutes. That's it. The block dissolves, the seaweed rehydrates, the beef fat renders into the broth. Done.

Honestly? This version is 90% as good as from-scratch. The sesame oil + beef sauté step is what makes it — that's the flavor foundation. The block handles the rest.

Tips for the Best Miyeokguk

  • Use soup soy sauce (gukganjang) if you can find it — it's lighter in color and saltier than regular soy sauce, keeping the broth clear
  • Don't skip the sesame oil sauté step — it's what gives the soup its signature nutty depth
  • The soup tastes even better the next day as the flavors meld overnight
  • For a lighter version, substitute beef with shrimp or clams — coastal Korean families often make it with seafood
  • Some families add a beaten egg in the last minute for extra richness
  • Miyeok is available at any Asian grocery store — look for the dried, dark green sheets in the seaweed aisle
  • Best beef cuts for miyeokguk: Brisket (the traditional choice — rich, fatty, melts into the broth), flank steak, or chuck. Skip lean cuts like sirloin or tenderloin
  • Always serve with white rice. Freshly steamed short-grain rice is the perfect pairing — Koreans either spoon rice into the soup or eat them side by side. The soup without rice is incomplete
